Reflection

What insights have you gained from setting SMART goals?

Setting a specific target — 85% — made a real difference compared to just telling myself "I want to do better." Having that exact number gave me something to measure against every time I got a mark back. I started actually paying attention to my grades in a way I hadn't before, and it helped me figure out which subjects needed more time.

How has it impacted your ability to achieve both individual and team goals?

For my own work, tracking marks weekly kept me honest — I couldn't ignore a bad mark because I was already checking in. For group work, like the Gemtime Watches project or the business venture, having a personal discipline mindset made me show up more organized and take my role more seriously. When you're accountable to yourself, you're naturally more accountable to the team.

Specific examples of how SMART criteria helped you stay focused and motivated?

The measurable part helped the most. Seeing that my Communication Technology mark was 92% motivated me to keep that up, while seeing Business at 65% told me I had real work to do there. The time-bound deadline of June 2026 also stopped me from saying "I'll do it later." When there's a real date, everything feels more urgent.

Best Work

Classroom Tasks

Real-World Business Venture  ·  Group Project
Darts, Basketball & Carnival Fest
⭐ Featured Work

This was the most hands-on assignment of the year — our group actually ran a real business in the cafeteria. We designed carnival-style games, set prices, managed a budget, and dealt with everything that came up on the day. My specific contributions included coming up with the original game concept, writing the entire risk assessment (which covered everything from a dartboard falling to competition from other vendor groups), and handling all the accounting, including the final income statement.

We spent $41.53 in total and made $37.35 over two days, ending with a net loss of $4.18. We had a tiered reward system — solo players could win a small reward, and groups of three or four who won together could keep all three prizes — which was designed to bring in more players. However, we bought more prize types than necessary (five instead of the three I suggested), which pushed us over the $40 budget and cut into our margins. We also lost our prime cafeteria spot to another group and didn't advertise as aggressively as I had planned.

What I learned: Having a good idea is not enough — you have to get your team behind it. I suggested buying fewer prizes to keep costs down and advertising more aggressively to pull in customers, but when my teammates went a different direction, I let it go too easily. That's on me as much as it's on the team. Real entrepreneurship means advocating for your plan and convincing people, not just suggesting it once. If we had followed the original plan, we could have made a profit of around $70.

Business Analysis  ·  Case Study
Porsche Case Study — Market Segmentation & Strategy
Checked in Class

This six-page case study had me analyze Porsche's business strategy in depth. I answered six detailed questions covering market segmentation, psychographic profiles, geographic targeting strategies, the launch of the Cayenne (a major business pivot for Porsche), and financial performance. I backed up my analysis with real data — including a 17% profit margin and 237,778 vehicles sold globally.

This assignment pushed me to think like a business analyst, not just a student. Using actual numbers and real company history to support your argument is a skill I will need whether I'm pitching a business idea, presenting to a client, or applying to Rotman Commerce.
Hands-On Manufacturing Challenge
Airplane Challenge — Speed, Quality & Competition
Won Both Events

In this challenge, we had 30 minutes to design and build paper airplanes as a group. Any plane with a faulty design was immediately disqualified, which meant speed and quality had to go together — you could not sacrifice one for the other. Our group of four split into two pairs: Sneh and Harshul as one team, and Simroz and I as another. We won both the manufacturing speed competition and the throwing distance competition.

Why this mattered: in a real manufacturing or product business, if your product has a defect, the customer does not buy it. If your competitor builds faster, they get to market first. This assignment made that lesson real and competitive in a way that a textbook never could. Winning both events showed me that working smart under pressure is something I can actually do.

End-of-Year Reflection

Course Reflection

01

Best Piece of Work

My best piece of work was the Darts, Basketball & Carnival Fest business venture. Even though our group ended with a small net loss, this was the assignment where I applied everything from the course in real life — market research, financial planning, risk assessment, and problem-solving on the spot. I designed the game concept, wrote the full risk assessment, and handled all the accounting. The experience taught me that entrepreneurship is not about perfect execution. It is about understanding your numbers, learning from what went wrong, and doing it better next time. One thing I would change: I should have pushed harder to get my team on board with keeping costs lower and advertising more aggressively. We had the plan. We needed to follow it.

02

Assignment I Liked Most

The assignment I liked most was the airplane challenge. We had 30 minutes to build planes under real competition conditions — faulty designs were instantly out, and whoever built fastest and flew farthest, won. My pair won both categories. What made it stand out was that it felt like a real production scenario. In an actual manufacturing business, a defective product gets rejected, and a slower competitor gets to market first. Those lessons hit differently when you're racing against a clock and competing against classmates than when you're just reading about them.

03

Three Entrepreneurial Skills I Learned

1. Financial Planning and Cost Control
Through the business venture, I learned that profit is not just about making sales — it is about controlling what you spend. I wanted to buy only three types of prizes to stay well under budget, which would have left room for a real profit margin. When we spent more than planned, that margin disappeared. Managing money carefully from the start is a skill that matters in every type of business.

2. Time Management Under Pressure
The airplane challenge gave me this one directly. With only 30 minutes, you cannot afford to second-guess your design or slow down for perfection. You build, test, adjust, and move. That kind of disciplined time management — doing quality work without wasting a single minute — is something every entrepreneur needs when deadlines are real and competition is watching.

3. Team Communication and Leadership
During the business venture, I had a clear plan for how to run things more efficiently — lower costs, more advertising, a smarter prize setup. But when I brought it up and the team went a different direction, I let it go instead of making my case properly. What I learned is that leadership is not just coming up with good ideas. It is convincing people to get behind them. You have to explain the reasoning, show why it works, and actually bring people with you. That is the skill I am still building.

04

How Entrepreneurial Skills Will Help Me in the Future

After high school, I want to study at Rotman Commerce at the University of Toronto. After that, I plan to start my own online business — I want to build and sell AI-generated websites for businesses that do not have one yet. I would not do the design work myself. I would be the person who talks to clients, explains the service, closes the deal, and makes sure they understand what they are getting and why it is worth it.

Everything I learned in this course connects directly to that plan. Financial planning helps me price my services correctly and stay profitable. Communication skills help me pitch to clients and handle the back-and-forth of sales. And understanding what customers actually want — not just what they say they want — helps me position my service better than a competitor who just talks about features. Without this course, I would not know how to actually run that business.

05

One Thing I'm Still Learning

One thing I am still working to understand is customer psychology — why people actually decide to say yes or no to a product or service. When I'm running my web design business, I will need to know how to ask the right questions: What problem are they trying to solve? Why did they look for help now? What would make them choose me over someone else? Once I understand those answers, I can explain why my service is worth the price and why it is better than going somewhere cheaper. That skill — reading a customer and responding to what they actually need — is something I did not really study in the course, but it is one of the most important things I will need to build.

06

How School Skills Prepare Me for a Career

A lot of the skills I built this year are directly transferable. Time management — knowing how to work efficiently under a deadline — will matter whether I'm managing multiple clients or preparing for exams at Rotman. Organization is just as important: if I'm running my own business and handling several projects at once, keeping everything sorted and on time is what keeps things from falling apart. And communication — being able to explain an idea clearly, make a quick pitch, and get someone interested in what you're offering — that's a skill I'll use every single day, in any business or career I go into.

07

One Career That Uses Skills From This Course

A marketing manager is a career that would use almost everything I learned this year. In that role, I would need to understand what customers want, build campaigns that reach the right people, manage a budget, and work with a team to get things done on time. All of those things — financial awareness, communication, market research, and teamwork — came directly from BEP2O. The course did not just teach me business concepts; it gave me hands-on practice with the actual skills that role requires every day.
